I went walking this morning without a destination, which is the only real way to walk. The trees are beginning their annual performance — slow, deliberate, devastating. Each leaf a small surrender. I used to think autumn was about endings, but now I see it as the most honest season. It doesn't pretend that holding on is the only form of strength.

The path wound through a section of the park I'd never noticed, hidden behind a row of birches whose bark peeled like old love letters. There was a bench there, weathered and slightly crooked, and I sat and listened to nothing in particular. A bird I couldn't name. The distant suggestion of traffic. My own breathing, which I forget about for most of the day.

There's a plant on my windowsill that I've been watching for weeks. It grows so slowly that I can't see it move, but each morning there's a tiny, almost imperceptible difference. A new leaf unfurling. A stem reaching a fraction of an inch toward the light. It is endlessly patient in a way I aspire to be.

I think feeling alive isn't about the grand gestures — the skydives and the declarations. It's about noticing. The texture of bark. The temperature of the air against your skin. The fact that you are here, right now, breathing, and that this is, against all odds, enough.
