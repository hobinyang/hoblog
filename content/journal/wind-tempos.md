I've been listening to a lot of ambient music lately — the kind that fills a room without demanding your attention. It sits in the background like weather, shifting and changing in ways you only notice when you stop and pay attention. There's an art to making something that doesn't insist on being heard.

Today I opened the window and let the wind compose its own piece. It played the curtains like a harp, tapped the windowpane in irregular rhythms, and whistled through the gap under the door in a sustained, breathy note. If I could transcribe it, it wouldn't look like any sheet music I've ever seen. No time signature. No key. Just pure, unself-conscious sound.

I tried to play along on the piano, matching the wind's tempo, but it kept changing — accelerating into gusts, then settling into something almost silent. It was humbling. The best musicians I know have this same quality: a willingness to follow rather than lead, to listen before they speak.

There's a Japanese concept called *ma* — the space between sounds, the pause that gives music its meaning. I think about this often. The silence isn't empty; it's full of potential. Every breath between notes is a choice, a small act of trust that the next sound will come when it's ready.
