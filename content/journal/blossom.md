Spring arrived without announcement this year. One day the branches were bare and skeletal, and the next they were frothing with blossoms — pink and white and that particular shade of pale yellow that exists nowhere else in nature. It felt like the world had been holding its breath and finally exhaled.

I've been thinking about what it means to bloom. Not the Instagram version — curated and symmetrical and always in perfect light — but the real, messy, uncertain kind. The kind where you don't know if you're a flower or a weed until someone tells you, and even then you're not sure they're right.

My grandmother used to say that the most beautiful gardens are the ones left slightly wild. She'd let the chamomile grow between the pavers and the ivy climb wherever it pleased. "Perfection is boring," she'd say, pulling weeds with one hand and letting others grow with the other. She was the most inconsistent gardener I've ever known, and her garden was the most beautiful place I've ever been.

I think there's something to be learned from that. Growth doesn't follow a schedule. Blooming doesn't require permission. Sometimes the most radical thing you can do is simply allow yourself to become whatever you're becoming, without trying to control the shape of it.
