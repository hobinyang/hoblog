It is a quiet Tuesday afternoon when I find myself standing on the edge of the balcony, paralyzed by the sheer vastness of the gradient above. The blue today isn't just blue — it's a heavy, saturated indigo that seems to drip into the horizon, mixing with the pale peach of a departing sun. I often wonder if we spend enough time looking up, or if we are too concerned with the texture of the ground beneath our feet.

The clouds are moving with a deliberate, slow-motion grace. They remind me of the way thoughts drift when you stop trying to catch them. There is a specific kind of peace that comes from acknowledging your own insignificance in the face of the atmosphere. It's not a diminishing thought, but rather a liberating one. If the sky can hold such weight and still remain weightless, perhaps I can learn to carry my own heavy thoughts with a similar buoyancy.

I remember a composition I heard once — a series of rising chords that felt like light breaking through a canopy. I tried to replicate that feeling today, hummed it into the wind, but the wind had its own melody. It was sharper, more erratic. It didn't care for my tempo. There is something comforting about the indifference of nature. It doesn't ask for our permission to be beautiful; it simply is.

As the light fades, the indigo turns to a deep, bruised purple. The first star appears — not a bright spark, but a shy pinprick of silver. I realize that the sky isn't a ceiling, but an opening. We aren't under it; we are within it. And for a moment, the world feels soft, expansive, and entirely enough.
